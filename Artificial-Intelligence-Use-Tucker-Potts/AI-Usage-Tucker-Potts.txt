AI:

This is a really good question — you’re bumping into the *actual purpose* of the Strategy pattern.

Let’s clear the confusion:

---

### What your instinct is saying

> “Why do we need strategies when we already have `DeliveryPlan`, `DeliveryRequest`, etc.?”

That’s valid — it *feels* redundant at first.

---

### The key separation (this is the whole point)

Think of it like this:

* **`DeliveryRequest`** → *what is being requested*
  (distance, type, urgency, etc.)

* **`DeliveryPlan`** → *the result*
  (final cost, creature, seal, etc.)

* **`DeliveryCostStrategy` (+ variants)** → *how the cost is calculated*

---

### Why separate “how” from everything else?

Because the cost logic can change independently.

Instead of doing this (bad design):

```java
if (type == EXPRESS) { ... }
else if (type == CAREFUL) { ... }
```

You delegate:

> “Hey strategy, you decide the cost.”

---

### So what each class is doing

#### `DeliveryCostStrategy`

* The **default pricing algorithm**
* A valid, usable baseline

#### `ExpressDeliveryStrategy`

* Overrides behavior for faster/more expensive delivery

#### `CarefulDeliveryStrategy`

* Overrides behavior for fragile/special handling

---

### Where they are actually used

Somewhere (likely in a service or planner), you’ll see logic like:

* Choose a strategy based on the request
* Pass the request into the strategy
* Get a cost back

👉 That’s the only job of the strategy classes

---

### Why not just put cost logic in `DeliveryPlan`?

Because then `DeliveryPlan` would:

* know about *all* pricing rules
* become a giant conditional mess
* violate single responsibility

---

### Mental model (keep this)

> **DeliveryPlan = result**
> **Strategy = calculator used to produce part of that result**

---

### One sentence summary

The strategies exist so you can **swap out pricing algorithms without changing the rest of the system**.
